## Review of Related Literature, Studies or System

**Game Economics & Virtual Economies**

The conventional study of economic development has been grounded in classical and neoclassical theories of economics, represented in the literature by Marshall (1890) and Todaro and Smith (2020). Marshall's foundational theories on supply and demand, marginal utility, and market equilibrium have long shaped how we understand economic systems. While these other authors further the ideas of institutions, human capital, and policies for growth, they emphasize at the same time that economic development is not merely a process of resource accumulation; rather, it must be underpinned by good governance and redistribution to provide effective remediation against inequality. Although these theoretical frameworks apply primarily to physical economies, they constitute a useful foundation for understanding the dynamics of virtual economies, specifically within online-game and digital-space contexts.

Virtual economies, as first outlined by Castronova (2002), are the newest frontier in which these traditional economic principles of scarcity, trade, and currency exchange manifest digitally. Lehdonvirta (2014) extends this idea, demonstrating that virtual economies do indeed express similar market dynamics to physical economies but with notably unique characteristics, such as digital good and currency creation. These virtual spaces are arenas not only of economics but also of labor, underlined by the phenomenon of "gold farming" in developing countries, whereby individuals create virtual goods online for real-world profit (Richard, 2008). This new form of digital labor demonstrates how virtual economies can create incomes in low-income countries, signalling the globalization of economic practices and raising concerns about labor rights, exploitation, and sustainability in these informal markets. Applying traditional economic theories to virtual economies shows both potentials and issues related to economic development. On the one hand, these virtual economies can give rise to new forms of employment, digital skills, and international economic integration. However, they also create complex issues related to regulation, market governance, and the commodification of leisure. While Marshall's principles of market behavior and competition apply to the increasing digital goods market, virtual economies challenge our notion of value, since digital assets are often created without physical constraints, leading to new conceptions of scarcity and worth. As the real and virtual economies increasingly intersect, innovative approaches to economic theory, policy, and regulation will be needed as these virtual spaces continue to evolve and grow in importance within the global economy.

**Player Motivation & Engagement**

Motivation is a very crucial aspect in understanding human behavior, especially in contexts like video gaming, where engagement and participation depend a lot on psychological factors. Self-Determination Theory, as conceptualized by Deci and Ryan (2012), gives an all-encompassing perspective on motivation in various domains, including gaming. SDT considers motivation to emanate intrinsically or extrinsically, with intrinsic motivation emanating from satisfaction arising from the innate psychological needs of an individual-autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Video game players are usually intrinsically motivated when the game gives an opportunity for challenge, achievement, and personal growth. Deci and Ryan's meta-analysis (1999) discusses how, at times, extrinsic rewards may undermine intrinsic motivation, such as when points or virtual items are used in controlling contexts. This becomes a very important consideration in understanding how a game designer can balance rewards and player autonomy to keep the players engaged without diminishing intrinsic motivation.

The "flow" concept, as described by Csikszentmihalyi (1990), supplements the SDT framework by portraying the psychological state in which a person becomes completely involved in an activity. Within gaming, this flow is understood to be an optimal experience in which a gamer becomes deeply focused and intrinsically motivated to the point where there is a perfect balance between skill and challenge. This then may be the very state of flow that supports prolonged engagement with video games because individuals want to prolong feelings of mastery and enjoyment. Csikszentmihalyi's model of flow has been related to gaming contexts in which players indicated that at states of complete absorption, performance was heightened when games were designed to match their capabilities with challenges. This feeling of deep involvement reflects the intrinsic motivation described by SDT, emphasizing enjoyment of the task and personal achievement as facilitators of long-term player engagement.

The work of Przybylski, Rigby, and Ryan (2010) further develops the application of motivational theories to video gaming by developing a model of video game engagement based on SDT. Much of their research highlights the role of autonomy, competence, and relatedness in promoting gaming engagement. Players are more likely to stay engaged when their in-game actions feel meaningful, when they feel capable of mastering challenges, and when they relate positively to others. Their model shows that the motivation to engage in gaming comes not just from outside factors but from fulfilling key psychological needs. Similarly, Hoffman and Nadelson (2010) looked at what drives motivational engagement in gaming. They found that players’ ongoing interest in video games comes from both internal factors, like enjoyment and challenge, and external ones, like rewards and competition. This study suggests that motivational engagement in video gaming is complex. Both internal psychological needs and external rewards significantly shape the behaviors and experiences of players. In conclusion, motivational theories such as Self-Determination Theory and Csikszentmihalyi’s concept of flow offer insights into the dynamics of video game engagement. All three theories stress the need for a balance between intrinsic and extrinsic motivators to keep players involved and highlight the role of psychological needs in building long-term engagement. Further research by Przybylski et al. (2010) and Hoffman and Nadelson (2010) sharpens this understanding by showing that both game design and a person’s psychological state affect how and why players engage with games. Together, these studies suggest that motivation in playing video games will best be understood with a holistic approach-one that accounts for both the internal drive of the player and those external elements of the game itself.

**Anti-Exploit & Security Theories**

Becker's (1968) economic theory of crime is widely used to explain criminal behavior, both in the physical world and online, including within video games. Based on this theory, individuals decide on undertaking a criminal action by weighing its potential benefits against its potential costs, such as punishment. In online game cybercrimes, cheats or hackers might exploit a system for a number of reasons if the rewards are believed to outweigh the risks. Using Becker's model, game developers can increase the perceived cost of criminal activity through the use of deterrents such as increased sanctions and improved detection systems that reduce cheating and fraud.

Douceur's (2002) theory in his Sybil attack highlights the vulnerabilities of decentralized systems whereby a single entity can generate numerous pseudonymous identities to manipulate the system. In this case, that would include the creation of fake accounts to disrupt online gaming, thus affecting in-game rankings, markets, or other systems. Developers can therefore institute identity verification methods through MFA and behavior-based account monitoring. With Douceur's theory in application, the developers will be protecting the integrity of virtual economies and ensuring that a fair gaming experience is delivered by preventing malicious actors from achieving undue influence with sham accounts.

Yan and Choi (2002) discuss the security challenges of online gaming-cheating and data breaches-and identify the need for protecting both player information and in-game data. In light of this, they call upon game developers to adopt proactive system designs for security, harnessing such technologies as encryption and anti-cheat solutions. Newsome and Song (2005) further extend this through dynamic taint analysis, which monitors untrusted data as it flows through a system in search of real-time exploits. Together, these approaches provide comprehensive solutions for maintaining security in online games and support developers in finding and mitigating threats that may compromise the experience for players or the integrity of the virtual economy.


**Blockchain in General and in the context of Web3 Games.**

Chen and Bellavitis (2020) discuss how blockchain technology has come to empower the rise of decentralized enterprise models. According to them, blockchain decreases dependency on intermediaries; consequently, it makes peer-to-peer interactions more feasible and transparent in general. In Web3 gaming, for example, this is practically manifested in the way players own, trade, and sell digital assets, such as NFTs, in a decentralized manner. Blockchain allows players ownership over in-game assets, recorded on the public ledger, independent of centralized control by game developers or publishers. This power shift carries new economic opportunities and challenges conventional game economies, wherein ownership of in-game digital goods and assets was often centralized with developers themselves.

Qin et al. (2023) present a review on DAOs in Web3 ecosystems, including architectures, models, and mechanisms. Fundamentally, DAO is a governance system that takes advantage of blockchain and smart contracts to provide decentralized decision-making power among stakeholders. Based on Web3 gaming, it outlines a more democratic and transparent way of governing games in which players could vote for updates to the game, the distribution of rewards, or community initiatives for direct control of the self-governing development and economic systems of the game. Developers will be able to implement more player-oriented and open environments for gaming by increasing engagement among players and giving shared ownership of the game to them.

Serada, Sihvonen, and Harviainen (2021) extend the argument regarding the impact of blockchain on the digital economies within games through the case of the CryptoKitties game, where blockchain brings ownership, value, and scarcity of digital assets. With blockchain, gamers can own verifiable, unique assets-including NFTs that can be traded, sold, or collected outside the confines of a single game. A new kind of "ludic economy" emerges, in which goods within the virtual world possess real-world value, while allowing players to take profits and participate in a much larger digital economy. Blockchain brings the aspect of scarcity into the virtual world, which is virtually unachievable in regular gaming, where goods are digitally plenty and very often not tradable. This opens new economic possibilities for players and developers in the realm of Web3. Iansiti and Lakhani (2017) give a broader view of the potential of blockchain to transform many sectors, which include gaming. They noted that it will reduce transaction costs, build trust, and eliminate the role of any intermediary because it gives an indelible record of transactions. In this case, the transparency afforded in Web3 games allows for secure in-game transactions, establishes decentralized economies, and develops DAOs wherein players are onboarded into a trustless system where everything is recorded on the blockchain, thus making ownership, trading, and rewards secure and non-repudiable. This shift toward decentralized and transparent systems is a big jump from the conventional model of gaming, promising a more participatory and equitable manner of playing. From what has been clear, the future of Web3 gaming lies in blockchain technology and DAOs. The idea of Christensen on disruptive capability provides a way in which blockchain is harboring a new business model with the potential to disrupt traditional centralized models in game development and publishing. Chen and Bellavitis (2020), on the other hand, describe how players are empowered through decentralized finance and peer-to-peer systems. Qin et al. (2023) explore the role of DAOs in forging decentralized governance and players' control. Serada et al. (2021) demonstrate how blockchain brings value, ownership, and scarcity to digital games, therefore creating a new "ludic economy." Finally, Iansiti and Lakhani (2017) describe the wider implications of blockchain for fostering a secure and transparent environment in decentralized systems. These put together create a profound understanding of the role of blockchain and DAOs in driving innovation and change within the Web3 gaming industry to more player-driven, transparent, and equitable virtual environments.
